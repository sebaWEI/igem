iGEM Special Prize Rules Explained

Special Prizes in iGEM are categorized in two main groups:

Grand Prizes

Teams are ranked by total score, selecting the top three Undergraduate, two High School, and two Overgraduate teams for the finals

From the three Undergraduate finalist teams, winners will be awarded Grand Prize, First Runner-Up, and Second Runner-Up

Village Awards

Teams are ranked against others within their specific village

Awards are divided between Collegiate and High School sections

  • For Collegiate teams: If both Undergraduate and Overgraduate have more than 10 teams, each receives a separate award
  • If either Undergraduate or Overgraduate has fewer than 10 teams, one combined Collegiate award is given

Award Judging Criteria

Judges evaluate based on three categories:

  • General Project
  • Presentation
  • Wiki

Each question is scored on a 6-point scale:

  • Poor work done or completely absent
  • Less work done; multiple missing components
  • Some work done; incomplete
  • Good work; complete
  • Great work; complete with some impressive components
  • Amazing work; impressive

Key Evaluation Criteria for Therapeutics Village:

Project completeness and feasibility
Ability to answer judges' questions
Data supporting conclusions
Project's potential impact
How Human Practices supported project development
Project promotion and social significance
Student participation level

iGEM Village Awards

The Village Awards recognize top teams in each Village category. Teams compete against others focused on similar applications of synthetic biology.

Award Structure

  • Teams are ranked against others in their village
  • For collegiate teams: At least 10 teams in each section (undergrad/overgrad) needed for separate awards
  • High School teams compete in combined village groups (starting 2025)
  • Judged based on Project, Presentation and Wiki scores

Why Villages Matter

  • Encourages teams to focus on specific challenges and applications
  • Creates communities of practice in specialized fields
  • Allows for fair comparison between similar projects
  • Recognizes excellence in specific domains of synthetic biology

Spotlight: Heidelberg - PICasSO

2024 Undergraduate Grand Prize & Best Foundational Advance Project

Project Overview

Heidelberg's team developed PICasSO (Programmed Interactions with Cas Staples), a groundbreaking platform that enables efficient programming of DNA interactions in mammalian cells.

The technology uses Cas proteins as "staples" to create complex biological connections and functional interactions, addressing fundamental challenges in synthetic biology circuit design.

Key Innovations

  • Created a modular system for predictable biological connections
  • Successfully validated Cas staples in mammalian cells
  • Developed methods for efficient delivery of large constructs
  • Integrated human practices to ensure responsible innovation

Why It Won

  • Exceptional engineering principles and design-build-test cycles
  • Strong supporting data and experimental validation
  • High potential for broad impact in synthetic biology
  • Outstanding presentation and documentation

Spotlight: Marburg - Tarakate

2024 Overgraduate Grand Prize & Best Agriculture Project

Project Overview

Marburg's team developed Tarakate, a groundbreaking project focused on transitioning from rainforest-destructive rubber production to sustainable rubber harvested from dandelions.

The project leverages plant synthetic biology to engineer dandelions for improved rubber yield and easier harvesting, offering an eco-friendly alternative to traditional rubber production methods.

Key Achievements

  • Established four efficient transformation protocols for dandelion
  • Successfully performed both transient and stable transformations
  • Developed comprehensive models for rubber production efficiency
  • Created a sustainable alternative to environmentally destructive practices

Why It Won

  • Addressed critical environmental sustainability challenges
  • Exceptional technical execution and experimental results
  • Strong real-world application potential
  • Third Grand Prize for Marburg since 2018, an unprecedented achievement

Spotlight: GEMS-Taiwan - Dengue Beeters

2024 High School Grand Prize & Best Infectious Diseases Project

Project Overview

GEMS-Taiwan created Dengue Beeters, an innovative solution to combat dengue fever by targeting mosquito populations through synthetic biology approaches.

The project combines recombinant biolarvicidal toxins with beetroot peel attractant to create Taiwan's first sticky ovitrap, aiming to significantly reduce mosquito populations and dengue transmission.

Key Innovations

  • Developed effective biolarvicidal toxins targeting mosquito larvae
  • Created sustainable attractant from beetroot peel waste
  • Designed Taiwan's first sticky ovitrap for mosquito control
  • Combined approaches for an integrated pest management solution

Why It Won

  • Addressed a critical global health challenge
  • Exceptional scientific rigor for high school level
  • Innovative integration of multiple approaches
  • Clear potential for real-world implementation

Mosquito Control

Sustainable Materials

Disease Prevention

Local Implementation

Project Impact & Vision

The team's motto: "Dengue Mos-quit-o by 2030!" reflects their ambitious goal of significantly reducing dengue fever cases in Taiwan and potentially worldwide through sustainable, accessible mosquito control methods.
